Maintaining regular school attendance

<< Going to school

Positive attendance habits help young people to keep up with school and maintain high levels of school attendance. There are many things families can do at home to help ALL young people prepare for school and go to school consistently.

Here are some ideas on what you can do to get your child to school and to stay in school.

Hold high educational expectations

Hold high levels of expectations toward education. This includes their learning, attendance, homework and goals.

  • Minimise school absences. If your child must miss school due to a health appointment, can they attend at least some hours of the school day?
  • Monitor school attendance regularly from the start of the school year.
  • Develop children’s interest in learning. Read to them, show curiosity in their learning.
  • Provide support with homework.
  • Encourage participation. In school activities, camps, sports, events, etc.

Show positive attitudes toward school.

  • Speak positively about school to children. This may help shape their attitudes towards school and beliefs about their attendance more positively.
  • Set long-term goals.

Foster good family relationships

Building a good relationship with your children can allow you to better understand them, including their preferences, strengths, goals and school experiences. and spot the signs they may be struggling. And help you to decide when to act.

  • Regularly check in with each other.
  • Listen to your child’s day.
  • Keep communication open.
  • Give uninterrupted time. Minimise distractions and give children your full attention when spending time together (e.g. talking, doing an activity together).
  • Celebrate your family's achievements, large and small.
  • Have conversations about school.
    • (when is a good time to talk?)
    • know what they like about school, interests, motivations, goals, what they are learning in school, areas that might be challenging and how you could support them.

Stay healthy

Staying physically and mentally healthy helps children to attend, take part in and keep up with school.

  • Keep up with vaccinations.
  • Handwashing.

Connect with the school

Your child’s school is now YOUR community. The school community, including teachers, school staff and other parents, is a valuable source of support for your child, yourself and your family.

Contact the school immediately when you have any questions about your child or require support - you do not need to wait till something serious or big ‘enough’ happens. Sometimes, parents may feel unsure about the ‘relevance’ of their questions. Even if schools do not have the right answers, they might be able to connect you to others who do. While a child may appear to be doing well, regular check-ins help families to understand how a child is actually faring and if there are emerging areas of concern.

You can connect with the school community by:

  • getting involved with school activities and committees
  • communicating openly and regularly with teachers and school staff
  • reading all school communication, e.g. newsletters, bulletins, e-mails
  • attending school events, e.g. performances, sports day, open day fairs.

Not all of these suggestions might be possible depending on your work and living situations.

Get good sleep

Sleep is important for our learning and development, physical and mental health. A lack of sleep may prevent young people from waking up on time or attending school.

ALL children and adults benefit from good sleep habits. These are actions we can take at home to help us have a good night’s sleep. To promote good sleep in children, establish healthy sleep habits early and stick to them throughout the week - even on weekends! Follow these sleep tips yourself and be a good role model for your child.

Sleep readiness

Throughout the day,

  • wake up and go to bed (e.g. 8pm) at the same time every day
  • avoid caffeine and high sugar after 3pm (often in chocolate, tea, coffee, Coke)
  • promote exposure to natural morning light to regular body clock.

Bedtime routine

Set a calm and sleep-inducing bedtime routine. In the lead up to bedtime,

  • plan 3 to 4 wind-down activities (e.g. dinner, bath, listen to relaxing music, read, go to bed)
  • provide an consistent and predictable order of activities each night
  • avoid stimulating activities 1 hour before bed, such as intense exercise, computer games
  • reduce screen time 1 hour before bed (e.g. TV, tablets, computers, smartphones).
Learn bedtime routines

Help your child learn bedtime routines, or implement changes to a routine, by using sleep stories and visual sleep schedules.

You can read to children social stories about sleep during the day, to help them learn about getting ready for bed or managing their worries about sleep.

Visual schedules use objects, drawings or pictures to break down tasks that have multiple steps.

A visual schedule for sleep can provide children with consistent steps and reminders for carrying out their bedtime routines.

Sleep conducive environment

Make sure the bedroom is cool, quiet and relatively dark. Tools to reduce light (e.g. blackout curtains, eye masks) or noise (e.g. earplugs) may be helpful.

Sleep problems are common among children with neurodevelopmental disorders. See a GP or paediatrician if you have concerns with your child’s sleep.

For more information about sleep and other sleep strategies, visit Sleep with Kit.

Be prepared for school

Get ready for school in advance. Check timetables, pack school bags and other school gear such as uniforms and sports equipment, lunch and snacks, allowance, the night before school.

Getting ready for school in advance helps young people to leave home and arrive in school on time, and feel confident knowing that they are prepared for the day ahead of them. Routines provide structure and let children know what to expect each day and what is coming up next.

Establish a morning routine for:

  • getting up
  • getting dressed
  • having breakfast
  • using the toilet
  • leaving the house.

An after-school routine may include having a snack, going to extra-curricular activities, doing homework, checking timetable, packing school bag, organising next day’s school essentials (e.g. uniform, sports gear, lunch and snack, allowance).

Create space to unwind

Young people may need time and space to unwind after the school day. They may like to:

  • listen to music
  • play games
  • talk with friends
  • take a bath
  • watch a video
  • create art
  • play sport.

Spending time in nature is also a great way to relax and bring a sense of inner peace.

You could suggest that the whole family do some of these activities together too.

Other ways to help

Understand the child factors and experiences that promote positive school perceptions.

Children may have worries about school or other sleep and mental health issues that prevent them from sleeping or attending school. Seek support from professionals early
