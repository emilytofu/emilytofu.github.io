Understanding missing school

< Going to school

If your child misses school, it's important to get them back to school as soon as possible. Find out why being absent from school matters, what your responsibilities are, and when irregular attendance may indicate bigger school attendance challenges.

Every day counts

There are situations when young people miss school, including:

  • Being late
  • Leaving early
  • Attending classes selectively
  • Illness or events that prevent them from attending.
Every day counts

Every day counts and there is no ‘safe’ threshold for absences.
– Hancock et al., 2013

Small amounts of absences add up to lost time in the classroom. Being away from school for 1 day a fortnight adds up to missing 4 whole weeks of school a year.

Is it ever ok to miss school?

Research shows missing school seriously disadvantages young people in many ways. They miss out on learning, participating in school events, excursions and interesting experiences with their friends, socialising, having fun and developing good habits that help them to succeed in future.

There are few, unavoidable situations for children missing school, such as sickness, school approved events including excursions and work experience, a death in the family, Koorie cultural absences (including Sorry Business) or an exemption from attending school.

If your child needs to miss school, let their school know as soon as possible. Tell them when and why your child can't come to school beforehand, on the day of absence (e.g. in the morning), or as soon as practicable after your child returns to school. Children who were ill should resume school as soon as they are well enough to do so.

However, many school absences are avoidable. Avoid keeping children away from school, having them arrive late or leave early for reasons such as:

  • Birthdays
  • Shopping
  • Visiting family and friends
  • Looking after other children
  • Sleeping in
  • Minor check-ups or care (e.g. hair cuts).

Medical appointments

Make routine medical or other health appointments for children outside of school hours (e.g. before or after school, during school holidays). If this is not possible, tell your child’s school before the appointment that they will not be at school during that time. Aim to have children attend at least some part of the school day if this is feasible for you or your child.

Family holidays

Take holidays during school holidays and not during term time. Discuss with your child’s school in advance if there are holidays planned for during school time. The school will decide if it is okay for your child to miss any school for a holiday. Work together to identify ways to help your child keep up with school. They might give your child work to do so they can remain engaged with their learning while away. Your child may share updates of their travels using photos or emails with their classmates.

Is it ever ok to miss school?

In general, it’s always better to go to school than not go.

Before allowing your child to miss school, consider the following:

  • “Are there alternatives to my child missing school?”
  • “Can this take place on another day or time?”
  • “Is it a good time for my child to miss school?”
  • “Will they miss out on learning, experiences or events that may set them behind their peers?”

If you’re not sure whether your child can miss school, ask the school.

Missing too many days

Whether children and young people miss school for a few days or longer, take all absences seriously to help them succeed.

Being absent from school for long periods means young people miss out on sharing in the school experiences with friends, socialising and making memories. They also lose momentum with their learning, routines and friendships. All of these can make it challenging for some young people to return to school after a long absence.

If you notice that your child’s school attendance is impacting on their mood and functioning or your wellbeing and employment, or if you have any concerns about their absence from school, then it is worth investigating the problem immediately. Find out more in the section slipping school attendance.

Some children may be absent from school for long periods of time due to chronic illnesses. Speak to their school about ideas to help. This could be making plans with the school nurse to manage a health condition and symptoms, or exploring alternative learning options to keep up with their learning.

Ways you can help

Attendance patterns are established early – children regularly missing days in kindergarten or in the early years of school often continue to miss classes in the later years. Help your child set positive school attendance habits early and maintain regular school attendance.

Young people who miss school frequently may be experiencing school attendance challenges that prevent them from going to school. It is helpful to recognise the common signs in young people, so that you can provide support promptly. Recognising common signs of school attendance challenges
