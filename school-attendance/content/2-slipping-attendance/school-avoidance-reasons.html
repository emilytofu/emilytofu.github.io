School avoidance reasons

Young people may feel distressed about attending school for many reasons. Being aware of them can provide you with a starting point towards understanding what might be happening for your child. Consider if any of the following applies to your child. Recognising and managing these factors may prevent school reluctance or more serious school attendance challenges from developing or persisting.

Child

  • Periods of school absence (e.g. due to illness or holidays)
  • Prior weak attendance (e.g. in early childhood, earlier school years)
  • Fear of failure, low self-esteem
  • Traumatic experiences
  • Hygiene or uniform concerns

Health conditions

  • Sleep problems and more generally, lack of sleep or poor sleep
  • Diagnosed and undiagnosed mental health problems
  • Physical illnesses
  • Chronic illnesses
    • For children who have chronic or serious illnesses and missed school regularly or for long periods, attending school may be challenging when they are physically well. They may fear bullying about their health conditions or time away from school, or worry about their ability to socialise, learn and participate in school. check with Missing School people

Academic concerns

  • Academic demands are too high (i.e. not keeping up with school work or specific subjects) or too low (i.e. losing interest)
  • Completing assigned homework
  • Test and exam stress
  • High achievement expectations from self, parents or school

Anxiety and worries

  • Separation anxiety
  • Worries about family members
  • Anxiety about school

Home and family

  • Family stresses (e.g. unemployment, housing/homelessness, domestic violence, death in the family)
  • Changes in family dynamics (e.g. conflict, parental separation)
  • Financial difficulties (e.g. money for food, transportation, school books, uniforms)
  • Caregivers' own experiences (e.g. history of school refusal, mental health problems, trauma, racism)
  • Home location (e.g. geographical remoteness, distance to school, access to affordable transport)
  • Attitudes toward school (e.g. low expectations toward school attendance, asked to stay home to assist with caregiving)
  • Parenting practices (e.g. low parental involvement, low supervision, differing parental responses to school attendance difficulties)
  • Lack of understanding between family and school (e.g. poor relationship, cultural differences)

School

  • Starting at a new school or level at school. Normal school moves, in particular transition to high school is related to dramatic decline in attendance, homework completion and studying.

School environment

These affect social interactions, friendships, emotional wellbeing, behaviour regulation and educational progress.

  • Experiences of discrimination
  • Feeling unsafe
  • Accessibility challenges, such as moving around school grounds, playing in school yard, using the toilet
  • Specific sensory experiences (e.g. sounds, light, touch, smells, pain)
  • Unexpected changes (e.g. new teachers, changes in routine)
  • Unprepared for transitions (e.g. switching tasks, activities or classes)
  • Learning and attention skills (e.g. persistence, concentration, memory, organisation skills) supports needs
  • Language and communication skills (i.e. ability to understand, process and use spoken and written language)

Relationships

  • Problems with peers (e.g., difficulty forming and maintaining friendships, peer pressure or conflict with peers)
  • Experiencing bullying (including verbal, physical and cyberbullying)
  • Poor student-teacher relationships
