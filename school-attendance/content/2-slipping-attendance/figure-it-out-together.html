Figuring out what is going on

As soon as your child shows signs of difficulty with school attendance, investigate what’s going on for them and around them, to understand their reasons for not attending. This can help you to know the types of support they need and identify your next steps.

Thinking about my child

Think about possible reasons that may cause distress and prevent your child from going to school. These can be situations or events that are new or ongoing, and happen to them or around them. Consider if there are:

  • Things that make it challenging to learn or participate in class and activities. For example, following spoken instructions, handwriting, noise, inaccessible equipment or school grounds, feeling anxious and managing frustration.
  • Academic concerns, such as keeping up with school work, taking tests, talking in front of the class and completing homework.
  • Friendship and peer concerns, such as no one to spend lunchtime with, group exclusions, feeling isolated and bullying.
  • Changes at home, such as having an ill family member, moving house or parents separating.
  • School-related concerns including attending a new school or year level, having new/replacement teachers and classmates or managing routine changes (e.g. camps, excursions, sports day).

All of these can affect how young people think or feel about school and themselves.

Young people may have other reasons for avoiding school. Read about causes of school attendance challenges.

Whether the initial reasons are clear to you or not, it is best to talk with your child and hear directly from them. This may help you to confirm what you already know, identify what else might be affecting them and work out the exact underlying cause or the most important one (if there are multiple causes).

My child’s thoughts and feelings about school

Having young people open up to you about their thoughts and feelings about school can take time. They may find it hard to respond to direct questions about what’s going on. They may not be able to identify exactly what their feelings are and why they are feeling them.

Before you start

Before starting a conversation, ensure that all caregivers are working towards the same goals regarding the child's school attendance.

  • Consider time and place. Discuss your child’s feelings when there is plenty of time, privacy and no distractions. This can be in the evenings or at weekends, but not just before school. Have these discussions in a relaxed environment when everyone is calm and can think clearly.
  • Be patient and persistent. It may take time and several tries before your child opens up to you. If your child is not ready to share, make another time to check-in with them.
  • Use your child’s preferred mode of communication. Talking, drawing, writing or typing may help them to express themselves to you.
  • Provide safety and encouragement. Relieving difficult experiences can be distressing for everyone. Children may need time to think or calm down. Ensure them that it is ok to rest and encourage them to try again when they are able to.

Begin with your observations

Start the conversation broadly and gently, using open, non-judgemental questions or statements, rather than those with ‘yes’ or ‘no’ answers.

Tell your child that you noticed something is not quite right by saying something like:

  • "I noticed that you don't seem so happy going to school at the moment."
  • "I noticed that you have been feeling sick a lot lately."
  • "You have gone a bit quiet lately. I wonder if there is something going on for you."

Give your child time and space to think and respond. Encourage them to talk openly about what they are going through.

When they do, be curious about their experiences and avoid showing judgement.

It may also be helpful to ask questions about school.

Listen carefully

When young people express themselves, listen closely to what they say and show empathy for their feelings and experiences. This means putting aside your views and opinions to fully understand what they are going through and provide the comfort they need to navigate a difficult period.

  • Use active listening. Listen with your full attention and observe their non-verbal communication, such as body language, facial expressions.
  • Show that you are listening. Confirm your understanding by nodding, maintaining eye contact and repeating back to them, in your own words, what you have understood.
  • Acknowledge and express empathy. When young people share their feelings and opinions about school, show that you understand, even if you disagree with them. Instead, let them know that their feelings are valid, comfort and reassure them by saying:
    • “I know that you think/feel ____. These feelings will pass once you are in school and get to class”.
    • "I can't imagine how you are feeling, but I'm here to listen."
    • “I know it’s hard to go back to school after the weekend/mid term/summer.”
    • "It's okay to feel this way."
    • “It won’t always feel like this.”
    • “I understand how overwhelming this must be for you.”
Why is empathy important in communication?

By showing that you genuinely care and want to support your child, you:

  • build trust and strengthen your relationship
  • encourage open and honest communication with you
  • provide emotional support during difficult times
  • show understanding and acceptance toward their perspective
  • foster a safe and supportive environment.
  • Don’t dismiss or minimise their feelings. Even if their concerns seem like small things to you, or not a reason why you yourself wouldn’t go to school, they may feel big and are real to your child. Avoid phrases like:
    • "Don't worry, it'll get better."
    • "It could be worse."
    • "You're overreacting. It's not that bad."
  • Offer your full support. Throughout the conversation, assure them that you will work together with them to overcome what is concerning them.
    • “I know you can do this, you are strong and I am here for you”
    • “We will figure this out together."
    • “I can see how this is affecting you. Let’s work through it together.”
  • Ask how you can help. Your child may know what they need, even if they are unsure what or why they are feeling a certain way about school.
    • "What's one thing we could do to help you feel better?"

Talk about school

Encourage your child to tell you what school is like for them. Ask questions about school such as:

  • “What are your favourite / least favourite parts of school?”
  • “How are you managing with the school work?”
  • "What have you been doing at lunch time?"
  • "Sounds like everything sucks. Is there something that does not suck as much?"

Be curious about their experiences at school, including how they felt, what happened as those feelings occurred, and what they did next.

  • "When do these feelings happen?"
  • "What was going on?"
  • "What thoughts did you have?"
  • "How did you manage that?"

You could also ask imaginary questions about school. Their answers to these prompts may reveal challenges that your child is experiencing.

  • "What does a perfect school look like for you?"
  • "If you could change one/three thing(s) about school, what would it be?"

The more you know about the types of situations or challenges that affect your child, the more informed you are to make decisions about how best to support them.

Not getting a response?

Young people may not know the reasons for their school attendance challenges or may not be willing to talk about them.

  • Reassure them that you care, you know something is wrong and want to understand so you can help them
  • Doing something, such as drawing or taking a walk together, while talking to you may help them feel comfortable.
  • Suggest other ways of communicating with you. For example, say "send me a text" or "write me a note".
  • Remind them that they can approach you when they are ready to talk.
  • Let them know that you will check-in again. This shows your child that you care and they are important to you.

Still not getting a response?

If your child won't open up to you after repeated tries and you are concerned about what is going on, let them know that you are going to seek help. Add statement to avoid blaming child. Before doing so, you may wish to explore and agree with other caregivers on your next steps.

  • Speak to school staff.
  • See your GP for further advice. They can check if your child's physical and mental health explains their school attendance challenges.
  • Talk to an adult who knows your child well. They might be grandparents, other family members, a family friend or a parent of your child's friend.

If you booked an appointment to see the GP or speak to the school, communicate this with your child.

Other considerations

  • Speak positively about education and help your child to think and talk about what they like about school life (e.g. friends, PE, art, lunchtime), though it may be challenging for children to recognise any positives at this point. Despite their school attendance challenges, focus on maintaining your child's interests in school and most importantly, learning.
  • Record your observations. This includes the signs of school attendance challenges and when they occur, the days your child attends and doesn't attend school and the strategies that have been trialled.
  • You can keep an attendance diary to gather these information, track progress and identify any missing school patterns. It can also help you to explain the situation to school staff or other professionals.

Getting in touch with the school

You may or may not be able to figure out what your child needs straightaway.

After talking to your child, it is recommended that you speak to their school about your concerns and what you already know about your child's attendance challenges.

To figure out what's causing your child's distress toward attending school, you can ask their school to help you identify and address the causes. Find out how to raise a concern and seek support from the school.

You may feel let down by your child's school or angry about their experiences. Prioritising your own wellbeing will help you to better support your child through this challenging time. Looking after yourself

Address the causes

After talking to young people and listening to their concerns about going to school, you may have ideas on what is going on for them and their reasons for avoiding school. Seek their input on where you might provide support or how they would like the problem resolved, to support their attendance. [support-school-attendance|Supporting school attendance]]
