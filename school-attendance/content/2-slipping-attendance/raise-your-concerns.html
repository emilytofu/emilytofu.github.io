Raise your concerns

The school should be the first point of contact if your child has difficulty attending school. Often, talking with your child's teacher is the best way to start. Tell them what is happening and ask for their support. Contacting the school early will also reduce the chances of the problem becoming more severe.

Ask to speak to someone in the school. This may be your child’s:

  • class teacher
  • principal or vice-principal
  • year level coordinator
  • learning and wellbeing coordinator
  • guidance counsellor
  • a trusted teacher.

Let the school know your child

It takes time for teachers and other school staff to become familiar with all students. You can help the school get to know your child by sharing their preferences, strengths and abilities. This information lets teachers plan for ways to best engage and support your child's success in school.

Tell them your concerns

It's always okay to talk to the school about your child's wellbeing or learning.

However, it is best to make an appointment time with the school staff and tell them what you would like to discuss, so that you can meet and have sufficient time to talk about your concern.

  • Be clear about the issues you want to discuss. It may be helpful to write down what you want to say beforehand and bring your notes, such as a completed attendance diary with you to the meeting.
  • Tell the school what you know. Let them know about your child’s feelings and the specific things that they are finding difficult.
  • Ask what is happening at school. For example, has the teacher noticed anything that your child finds particularly challenging? Are there sudden changes in their school performance or behaviours?
  • Be open and honest. Share with the school any information that may provide clues about the reasons for your child’s school attendance. For example, changes at home or about family members.
  • Work with school staff to look for patterns in your child’s absences from school (e.g. certain days of the week or after lunch).

- escalation process

Keep in regular contact with your child’s teacher, or the main contact person at the school, about how your child is doing at school.

You are the expert of your child.

Tell the school if you would like an interpreter or a support person to come to the meeting with you.

Support person

You can have a support person to help you at any time while making a complaint.

The support person can be someone in your family, a friend, community member or from a support agency.

The support person can:

  • help you clarify the issues
  • discuss difficulties you're having about the complaint
  • help create a good working relationship between you and the school
  • help you understand our policies and any resolutions proposed.

Let the principal know you want to use a support person when making the complaint. You should provide their name, contact details and relationship to you.

Interpreter

An interpreter can support communication with the school and help you to relax and express yourself comfortably.

If you need an interpreter to talk to your school, call the National Translating and Interpreting Service on 131 450. For further information, please refer to the department's Interpreting and Translation Services page.

Ask teachers for ideas

Through school meetings, you may have figured out the barriers to your child's attendance.

You and your child may already have identified some things that might help them at school. You can also ask the teacher for their ideas on what changes or supports could be put in place to support their wellbeing and learning, or ways to work together.

  • arranging for a member of staff to be available in the morning to support a smooth transition into school.
  • the school could link your child with someone that checks in with them during the day, such as a peer buddy (this is a child the same age as your child), a trusted teacher, the school nurse, or another safe person.

If your child finds school overwhelming or makes them feel anxious provoking, perhaps during difficult moments they could get to a quiet space like a: • library • sensory room • nurture room.

Trial these ideas and monitor your child’s school attendance. If problems persists or worsens, find out how you access professional supports for your child. seek-additional-supports

Case studies

Case studies of reasons, feelings, thoughts and possible solutions?

Reason

Child has difficulty following spoken instructions and misses a piece of information given. Teacher yells at them for 'not paying attention' and being the only student to read the wrong text.

Feeling
I am confused about what to do in class because the teacher speaks too fast. I feel embarrassed for being yelled at and singled out in class. Thoughts I hate this teacher.

Possible solutions

  • Provide written and spoken instructions.
  • Simplify instructions. Speak clearly and give limited information each time. Check for understanding before moving on to the next step.
  • Check in with student to ensure understanding
  • Ask teacher to be kind, approachable and easy to talk to.

Escalation

If you are unhappy with the school's response

You can complain if you are unhappy with the support you or your child are getting. Contact your closest regional office for questions, feedback or assistance in working with the school.

contact the department's regional offices on 1800 338 663 or email enquiries@education.vic.gov.au

If you are unhappy with the regional office response

If you have received a response from the regional office, and are still unhappy with the outcome, you can contact the central office. The central office can also refer eligible complaints to the Independent Office for School Dispute Resolution.

You can contact our central office by completing an online form.

You can also contact the central office by:

  • emailing school.complaints@education.vic.gov.au
  • phoning (03) 8688 7885 (message call-back service)
  • mailing the below postal address

Deputy Secretary, Schools and Regional Services
c/o Manager, Complaints and Improvement Unit
GPO Box 4367 Melbourne VIC 3001

If you are unhappy with the department's response

If you are unhappy with the department's response, you can contact the Victorian Ombudsman by phone (03) 9613 6222 or via their website.
