Barriers to school attendance

Animation from child's POV

Duration:

All of the things around young people shape their ability to go to school - what happens at home, in the places they learn and play, in their neighbourhood and even in the bigger world. When they feel distressed about going to school, it is important to understand what is going on for them and around them.

[At night, Child 1 is in bed but has difficulty falling asleep, thinking about school.]

Worrying about school is common. Young people may feel stressed about friendships, an upcoming test or performance, or a subject or class. Worries may affect sleep and make it harder to go to school the next day.

[The next morning, child 1 misses morning alarm, wakes up late, feels cranky and tired. Gets ready for school slowly.]

When sleep is poor, young people may have difficulty waking up on-time for school. They may feel tired or cranky, or start their day feeling stressed and unprepared. Others may feel unprepared for school because they are concerned with maintaining personal hygiene like taking a shower and brushing their teeth, and having clean uniforms and shoes.

[Child 2 walks from bedroom to kitchen, house is tidy, baby crying in background. Sees parents arguing. Child is thinking about mum who looks tired. Child lets out a big sigh.]

Things that happen at home, to family members and in one's community have impacts on young people, just like how a young person's experiences may affect those around them too. Caregivers may have lots going on in their lives and feel stressed out. They might need extra help to cope but feel unsure where to go for information and support.

[Split screen. On the left, child 3 waits for bus that is late, rides bus to school for a long time. On the right, car arrives in front of school, child 4 cries in car and clings onto mother, unable to leave. Both children reach school late and are scolded by a teacher.]

Depending on how far away school is and ways to get there, the journey to school can be long and tiring, especially when living in regional and rural areas. Some young people, in particular young children, may feel fearful when separated from loved ones or being away from home.

[Teen enters school looking sad, back hunched, walks alone in school away from peers/bullies who are pointing and laughing, thinks about school (cross out pride flag, trans flag) that is not inclusive. Worries about cyberbullying.]

At school, some young people may not feel safe or accepted by their school community. They may experience discrimination or bullying because of their unique identities, appearances, abilities or experiences. There might be conflicts with peers or peer pressure to conform in order to feel like they belong. Avoiding school protects them from hurtful teasing and bullying, which can affect their self-esteem and wellbeing.

[Lonely child who uses mobility aid stands outside playground, can't enter due to steps and non-paved ground, watches peers play.]

The school environment can be challenging for many reasons. It may not be built for all young people. Parts of the school, facilities or equipment may be inaccessible, and prevent them from moving around independently, playing with their friends, taking part in sports and other activities.

[Chaotic, noisy and enclosed indoor sports court, bright lights, alarm goes off suddenly. Child stands in change room worried about changing into PE attire, can't cope with strong scents such as deodorants or cleaning products. Lockers slamming.]

Schools often lack sensory-friendly spaces, with areas being chaotic, noisy, bright, enclosed and unpredictable. These sounds, lights and smells, or even temperature and textures, can overwhelm those who prefer calm, familiar and low distraction settings.

[Teacher is writing on the white board in front of class, most students pay attention to the teacher. A child tries to concentrate but is confused. Eventually becomes bored, fidget in seat and gets distracted by what's happening outside the window. Feels embarrassed because they can't answer teacher's question, classmates laugh. Gets back test results and looks disappointed.]

In the classroom, teaching practices and the academic curriculum may have barriers that prevent young people from participating and learning. Young people may require support with reading, handwriting, numeracy and other important skills, like managing emotions or changes and communicating with others. When their needs are not met, they may feel frustrated, confused and bored in class. It can be stressful keeping up with school work and meeting academic expectations.

[Teacher stops to check-in on child. Child now understands and looks relieved and engaged. Happy child in classroom, surrounded by happy students and teacher.]

With the right supports in place and changes in home and school environments, young people can feel great about going to school. They can feel safe, join in at school, enjoy playing and learning with their peers.
